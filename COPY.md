## Hero
- EYEBROW: no robots-take-your-job hype
- H1: AI product marketing, minus the hype.
- BODY: AI didn't replace product marketing. It changed how fast one good PMM can move. I'm Divya Sadanandan, a fractional Head of Product Marketing, and I build AI into the work where it earns its place and keep it out where it quietly breaks things. Here's where the line is.

## What AI changes
- EYEBROW: The honest version
- H2: What AI actually changes in PMM.
- BODY 1: The research-to-message loop used to take weeks. Read the calls, sort the tickets, find the pattern, write the story, test it. AI compresses the mechanical parts of that into days. I can synthesize three hundred sales calls overnight, draft ten messaging angles before lunch, and spend the time that frees up on the part that actually moves growth: deciding which angle is true for your market and killing the other nine.
- BODY 2: That's the shift. Not fewer product marketers. More throughput per product marketer, and a higher bar for the ones who only ever did the mechanical parts.

## Workflows
- EYEBROW: In practice
- H2: The AI-native workflows I run.
- BODY: Specific, not magic. Each of these used to be a person-week. Now it's a day, and the output is better because I can look at all of the data, not a sample.
- WORKFLOW 1 TITLE: Research from real customer data
- WORKFLOW 1 DESC: Call transcripts, support tickets, reviews, and win/loss notes synthesized into the actual words your buyers use and the objections that keep recurring.
- WORKFLOW 2 TITLE: Messaging variants, built to test
- WORKFLOW 2 DESC: A spread of angles generated fast, then narrowed by what the market repeats back, not by what sounds clever in a doc.
- WORKFLOW 3 TITLE: Lifecycle and onboarding at scale
- WORKFLOW 3 DESC: Personalized flows and triggers that used to need a content team, shipped by one PMM with the right setup.
- WORKFLOW 4 TITLE: Enablement that keeps up with sales
- WORKFLOW 4 DESC: Battlecards, one-pagers, and objection handling produced as fast as the market moves, then checked by hand before they ship.

## Guardrails
- EYEBROW: Where it breaks
- H2: Where AI hurts product marketing.
- BODY 1: Positioning is a decision about what to say no to. AI averages. Point a model at your category and it gives you the most likely sentence, which is the same sentence your competitors get. That's how a whole market ends up sounding identical, and how you become invisible while shipping more copy than ever.
- BODY 2: So I keep AI on the inputs and the drafts, and keep a human on the call that matters: what you stand for, and the proof underneath it. AI also makes up numbers when you let it. Every claim that ships gets traced back to something real.

## AI FAQ
- EYEBROW: Straight answers
- H2: AI PMM, answered.
- Q1: What is an AI PMM?
- A1: An AI PMM is a product marketer who builds AI into the actual work: synthesizing customer research, drafting and testing messaging, running lifecycle and enablement at a speed a human team can't match by hand. The judgment stays human. The grunt work gets compressed from weeks to days.
- Q2: Can AI replace a product marketer?
- A2: No, and anyone selling you that hasn't done the job. AI is fast at the parts that are mechanical: summarizing calls, generating variants, sorting data. Positioning is a decision about what to say no to, and that's a judgment call AI can't make for you. What AI changes is how much one good PMM can get through.
- Q3: What's the difference between hiring a fractional PMM and buying AI tools?
- A3: Tools give you output. They don't tell you which output is right for your stage, your market, and the constraint capping your growth. I bring the tools and the judgment to use them, and I'm accountable for whether growth moves. A seat license isn't.
- Q4: Is AI-written marketing copy a problem?
- A4: It is when everyone runs the same prompts through the same models and ends up sounding identical. The market stops hearing you. I use AI to move faster on research and drafts, then make sure what ships sounds like you and stakes out a position, not the statistical average of your category.
